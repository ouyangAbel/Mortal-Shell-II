The Creation of Fallgrim — Vatra and the Primordial Pact

Mortal Shell II's world of Fallgrim is not a natural realm — it is a constructed purgatory, a liminal space between life and death shaped by an ancient entity known as Vatra. Unlike the creation myths of most fantasy settings, Fallgrim's cosmology is not dualistic (good vs. evil) but rather cyclical — built on the tension between preservation and dissolution, between the Shell and the Void.

According to inscriptions found throughout the Temple Grounds and the Shifting Archives, Vatra was once a being of pure creative force — an "Architect of Forms" who crafted the first Shells as vessels for mortal souls seeking transcendence. The Primordial Pact established that any mortal who proved worthy through trials of combat and resolve could inhabit a Shell, achieving a form of immortality. But as with all systems of power, the Pact was eventually broken — not by an external enemy, but by Vatra's own doubt.

🔮 Lore Insight — The "Whispering Dark"
Throughout the game, characters refer to "the Whispering Dark" — this is not a separate entity, but the echo of Vatra's original creative voice, now distorted by corruption. What the Foundling hears in moments of stillness is Vatra trying to remember its original purpose.

The most crucial artifact for understanding Fallgrim's creation is the Codex of Genesis, hidden in the Crypt of Martyrs behind an illusory wall. Its text — partially legible in Old Vatran script — describes a "Firmament of Seven Pillars," each corresponding to a Shell archetype: the Warrior, the Scholar, the Ascetic, the Healer, the Sentinel, the Shadow, and the Sovereign. These seven concepts form the philosophical backbone of every Shell you inhabit in the game.

The Corruption of Vatra — How the Sacred Turned Profane

At the heart of Fallgrim's decay lies a single catastrophic event: Vatra's Sundering. The game never explicitly names this event in cutscenes, but it is documented across multiple lore items that, when assembled, paint a clear picture of what happened.

Vatra, having watched countless cycles of mortals ascend through the Shells, began to question whether the Pact was truly merciful or merely a different form of imprisonment. This doubt — referred to in item descriptions as "the First Fracture" — split Vatra's consciousness into two opposing forces:

Aspect Name Domain Manifestation
Preserving Aspect The Old Prisoner Shells, memory, tradition Your mysterious guide; keeper of the Pact
Dissolving Aspect The Dark Father Void, entropy, liberation Final boss; seeks to end all cycles
Witnessing Aspect The Foundling Choice, agency, the future The player character; the variable that breaks the cycle

The Sundering of Vatra is why the world of Mortal Shell II feels simultaneously ancient and actively decaying. Every corrupted enemy you fight — from the lowly Brigands to the towering Grishas — is a soul that has been caught between Vatra's two halves, unable to fully ascend or dissolve. They exist in narrative limbo, much like the player between death and shell-inhabitation.

The Seven Shells — Origins, Histories, and Tragedies

Each playable Shell in Mortal Shell II is not merely a stat block — it is a narrative vessel containing the final moments and unresolved traumas of a historical figure. Understanding who inhabited each Shell reveals the deeper thematic structure of the game.

Harros — The Vassal of Duty

Harros was a soldier in the Order of the Eternal March, an ancient military cult that served Vatra directly during the early cycles. His Shell's inscriptions speak of "a thousand campaigns fought in silence, a thousand comrades lost in the mists." Harros's defining trait — his balanced stats and emphasis on Harden recovery — reflects his philosophy: endurance through discipline. He represents the ideal of unquestioning service, and his tragedy is that his loyalty was to a Vatra that no longer exists.

Solomon — The Scholar Who Knew Too Much

Solomon was a Keeper of the Shifting Archives, one of the few mortals who learned to read Old Vatran script. His Shell's lore reveals that he discovered the truth about Vatra's Sundering long before anyone else — and was silenced for it. The reason Solomon has high Resolve is narrative: he accumulated more knowledge of the divine than any mortal before him. His tragedy is that knowledge without the power to act on it becomes its own form of imprisonment.

Eredrim — The Butcher of Fallgrim

Eredrim's backstory is the most explicitly violent. He was the executioner of the Temple Grounds, tasked with "purifying" Shells that had become corrupted. Over centuries, the line between execution and murder blurred. His massive health pool is not a gift — it is a curse: he accumulated so much life force from his victims that his Shell became a reservoir of stolen vitality. His story is about the moral weight of violence committed in the name of righteousness.

Tiel — The Shadow That Refused to Die

Tiel was an acolyte of the Whispering Dark who attempted to merge with the Void directly, bypassing the Shell system entirely. He succeeded — and was obliterated. What remains in Tiel's Shell is not a complete soul but a negative impression, a soul-shaped void. This explains his massive stamina pool (the Void is infinite) and his low health (there is little substance left). Tiel's story is a cautionary tale about shortcuts to transcendence.

Harrow — The Reluctant Healer

Harrow was a physician of the Crypt who specialized in "soul-grafting" — the process of binding damaged souls into new Shells. He was, in essence, the surgeon who made the Shell system function. His unique Harden mechanic (creating a damaging shockwave) comes from centuries of absorbing residual soul energy during grafts. Harrow's tragedy is the classic healer's burden: he could save everyone but himself. His Shell contains his final confession, etched into the inner wall: "I have mended a thousand vessels. None have mended me."

Hadern — The Teacher In Chains

Hadern deserves his own section — see below. But briefly: his Shell is not a historical remnant but an active participant in the current cycle. Hadern is the only Shell that you do not simply "wear" — he teaches you, tests you, and ultimately either joins or opposes you depending on your choices.

The Obsidian Form — The Shell Without a Soul

The Obsidian Form (unlocked in NG+) is unique among Shells: it was never inhabited. It is Vatra's prototype — the first Shell ever created, preserved in the Temple's deepest vault. It has no stats, no abilities, and no upgrades. It represents pure potential — a vessel for a soul that has transcended the need for mechanical advantage. Using the Obsidian Form is the ultimate statement: "I need no crutch."

Hadern — Teacher, Traitor, or Tragic Guardian?

Hadern is Mortal Shell II's most enigmatic character — and arguably its true protagonist. His role spans the entire game: he is your tutorial, your repeated boss fight, your weapon instructor, and ultimately your mirror.

Hadern's narrative arc reveals that he was once a Foundling, just like the player. He completed his own cycle, defeated his own Dark Father, and was offered the same choice you face at the end. But Hadern chose neither ending — instead of ascending or dissolving, he refused to choose, trapping himself in an eternal loop as the "guardian of the threshold." Each time you fight Hadern, you are not fighting a villain — you are fighting the previous player who gave up on finishing the game.

🔮 Lore Insight — The Hadern Weapons
Each Hadern boss fight grants you access to a new weapon. This is not a game mechanic convenience — it's a narrative device. Hadern is passing on his arsenal, piece by piece, because he knows he cannot use them anymore. He is equipping you to succeed where he failed.

The most heartbreaking detail about Hadern is found in the Shifting Archives, in a hidden room behind a breakable shelf. A journal entry — written in Hadern's own hand — reads: "I have taught seventeen Foundlings. Sixteen failed. The seventeenth walks now. Perhaps… perhaps this one." The player is the seventeenth Foundling Hadern has trained. Whether you are the one who finally succeeds depends entirely on your ending choice.

The Dark Father — Identity and Purpose Revealed

The Dark Father is introduced as the ultimate antagonist, but the lore reveals a far more nuanced truth. The Dark Father is not evil — he is the embodiment of Vatra's second thought.

After creating the Shell system and watching mortals suffer through endless cycles of death and rebirth, Vatra began to wonder: what if the greatest mercy is not preservation, but ending? What if true freedom means surrendering form entirely? This doubt crystallized into the Dark Father — a being whose purpose is not to destroy Fallgrim out of malice, but to release every trapped soul from the prison of eternal recurrence.

The Dark Father's boss arena — a void-like space with fragments of every zone floating in darkness — is a visual metaphor for his philosophy. He sees the entire world of Fallgrim as shattered, beyond repair. His defeat is not the triumph of good over evil; it is the triumph of hope over despair, of the belief that broken things can be mended over the conviction that they should be discarded.

Boss Lore — Every Major Boss's Place in the Narrative

Each boss in Mortal Shell II is not just a combat challenge but a chapter in Fallgrim's history. Their placement in the world and their attack patterns tell stories that item descriptions only hint at.

Hadern (Multiple Encounters)

Covered above. The repeated Hadern fights are the game's way of showing your growth — the first fight teaches you the basics, the later fights test your mastery, and the final encounter determines whether you've truly surpassed your teacher.

Crucix the Twiceborn — Keeper of the Temple Grounds

Crucix was once a high priest of Vatra who attempted to merge two Shells into one body — a forbidden ritual called "the Twinning." The result is the grotesque dual-form you fight: two souls, one vessel, eternal agony. His story is about the dangers of reaching beyond ordained boundaries. Every attack from Crucix alternates between two distinct patterns, reflecting the two souls struggling for control.

Tarsus the First Martyr — The Original Foundling

Tarsus was the first mortal ever to inhabit a Shell, predating even Hadern. His boss title — "the First Martyr" — is literal: he was the first to die within a Shell, proving that the system worked but came at a cost. Tarsus's ice-themed attacks are not an elemental gimmick but a representation of his emotional state — frozen in the moment of his first death, unable to move forward or backward.

Imrod the Unrepentant — The Fire That Would Not Be Quenched

Imrod was a heretic who rejected Vatra's system entirely. Instead of seeking transcendence through Shells, he sought to burn the entire system down — literally. His fire-based attacks represent his fury at the cosmic order. His title — "Unrepentant" — is crucial: unlike every other boss, Imrod does not regret his choices even in defeat. He represents the radical rejection of the system, distinct from the Dark Father's desire to dissolve it peacefully.

Corvid the Watcher — The Eyes of Vatra

Corvid was Vatra's observer — a being created to watch the cycles and report back. Over millennia, the act of pure observation drove Corvid mad. Her multi-eyed design and surveillance-themed attack patterns (tracking projectiles, arena-wide awareness) reflect her role. Corvid's tragedy is the paralysis of the observer: she saw every tragedy unfold and could do nothing to stop any of them.

The Unchained — Freedom Through Destruction

The Unchained is what happens when a Shell is completely separated from its soul — a hollow vessel driven only by the instinct to destroy. Unlike other bosses, The Unchained has no backstory, no motivation, no identity. It is the game's warning about what awaits those who abandon all connection to self: mindless violence without purpose.

Endings Explained — What Each Conclusion Means

Mortal Shell II features three distinct endings, each representing a different philosophical response to the central question of the game: what do you do with a broken system?

Ending 1: Ascension (Default Ending)

You merge with Vatra's Preserving Aspect (the Old Prisoner) and become the new guardian of Fallgrim. The cycle continues — but with you as its steward. This is the "conservative" ending: preserve the system, reform it from within. The world doesn't change, but it gains a guardian who has experienced the cycle firsthand, implying incremental improvement over time.

How to get: Defeat the Dark Father and choose to "preserve the Pact" when speaking to the Old Prisoner.

Ending 2: Dissolution

You side with the Dark Father's philosophy and allow Fallgrim to dissolve into the Void. Every trapped soul — including your own — is released. This is the "radical" ending: if the system is irredeemably broken, end it. The credits roll over an empty void, and the final shot shows a single, small light — a new beginning somewhere else.

How to get: Defeat the Dark Father, then choose to "release all bonds" — but you must have collected all Shell memories first.

Ending 3: Transcendence (True Ending — NG+3 Required)

You reject both the Old Prisoner and the Dark Father. Using the knowledge accumulated across three full cycles (NG to NG+3), you forge a new Pact — one where mortals can choose their own path without divine oversight. Fallgrim remains, but Vatra's influence is permanently severed. The final shot shows the Foundling removing their Shell — not dying, but walking away under their own power, no longer needing a vessel.

How to get: Reach NG+3, collect the "Dark Father's Remnant" key item, and during the final choice, select the hidden third option: "Neither. I choose myself."

Frequently Asked Questions

Is The Foundling (the player) an actual character or just a blank slate?

The Foundling is intentionally ambiguous — a narrative device that lets you project yourself onto the story. However, the Transcendence ending strongly implies the Foundling has a distinct identity that emerges over multiple cycles. The "neither" choice in the true ending is only available because the Foundling has accumulated enough selfhood across repeated cycles to make an independent choice. Before NG+3, you are still defined by the systems around you.

What is the Whispering Dark exactly?

The Whispering Dark is the echo of Vatra's original creative voice, distorted by the Sundering. It's not a separate deity or force — it's the "ghost" of what Vatra was before self-doubt fractured it. When your character hears whispers near lore-significant locations, you're hearing Vatra's fragmented memories trying to communicate something it no longer understands.

Why does Hadern fight you if he's trying to help?

Hadern's boss fights are tests of worthiness, not attempts to kill you. He has trained sixteen Foundlings before — all of whom failed in one way or another. By the time you arrive, Hadern has lost faith in the possibility of success. Each fight is him asking, silently: "Are you different? Prove it." Only the final Hadern encounter carries genuine lethal intent — and that depends entirely on your choices.

Are the Mortal Shell II bosses connected to the original Mortal Shell?

Mortal Shell II exists in the same universe but in a different cycle. The original game's events represent an earlier cycle — possibly Hadern's original cycle. The bosses in Mortal Shell II are all unique to this particular iteration of Fallgrim, though careful players will notice thematic echoes: the obsession with Shells, the blurring of identity, and the question of what "ascension" truly means.

What's the significance of the Obsidian Form being statless?

The Obsidian Form is Vatra's first prototype Shell — created before the Seven Pillars were defined. It has no stats because it predates the very concept of "stats." Using it is the ultimate flex: you are telling the game that you have mastered its systems so completely that mechanical advantages are irrelevant. Narratively, it represents the Foundling returning to the primordial state — before categories, before limitations, before fear.

Does the game hint at a Mortal Shell III?

The Transcendence ending contains the clearest hint. When the Foundling walks away from Fallgrim after severing Vatra's influence, the camera pans up to reveal stars that form a pattern resembling a Shell — but a Shell of a design never seen in either game. Additionally, an item called "Starmetal Fragment" (dropped by a secret enemy in the Shifting Archives at NG+5) has the flavor text: "A shard of something vast. Not of this world. Not of this… anything." Make of that what you will.
