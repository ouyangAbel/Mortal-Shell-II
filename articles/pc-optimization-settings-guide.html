System Requirements — Minimum, Recommended & 4K Specs

Mortal Shell II runs on Unreal Engine 5, and while it is better optimized than many UE5 titles, the hardware floor is real. Before tweaking settings, make sure your rig meets at least the minimum bar. Below are the official and community-verified requirements.

Critical: Mortal Shell II requires an SSD. Running from an HDD causes 3–8 second texture-streaming hitches every time you enter a new zone or approach a boss arena. The game streams assets aggressively during traversal — an HDD cannot keep up with the I/O demand. If you only make one hardware change, make it this one.

Every Graphics Setting Explained — What to Turn Down First

Pro Insight: Shadow Quality and Global Illumination are the two heaviest settings — together they account for roughly one-third of your total GPU frame time. If you need a quick 25–30% FPS boost, drop Shadow Quality to Medium and Global Illumination to High. The visual difference in actual gameplay (not side-by-side screenshots) is negligible, especially during combat when your eyes are tracking enemy animations, not shadow softness.

Motion Blur and Depth of Field: Turn both OFF. Motion Blur adds ~2ms of frame-time latency and smears detail during camera rotation — exactly when you need visual clarity to read enemy tells. Depth of Field blurs distant enemies during lock-on transitions, which is actively harmful in a game where you are constantly swapping targets in multi-enemy encounters. The only argument for keeping them on is cinematic screenshots, and you can toggle them temporarily for that.

NVIDIA Control Panel Tweaks

These settings override the game's engine defaults and can significantly reduce input latency, eliminate micro-stutter, and improve frame pacing. Access via: NVIDIA Control Panel → Manage 3D Settings → Program Settings → Add Mortal Shell II (mortalshell2.exe).

Critical Tweaks

  • Low Latency Mode → Ultra: Reduces the render queue from 3 frames to 1. Input lag drops from ~35ms to ~18ms at 60 FPS — a night-and-day difference for parry timing. If you are struggling with parry windows, this is likely why.
  • Power Management Mode → Prefer Maximum Performance: Prevents GPU clock-speed down-throttling during low-intensity scenes (menus, dialog, walking between encounters). Eliminates the sudden frame-time spike when the GPU ramps back up for combat.
  • Texture Filtering — Quality → High Performance: Saves ~3% GPU load with no visible difference in a dark-toned game like Mortal Shell II. The difference is only visible in side-by-side pixel-peeping comparisons on bright, high-frequency textures.
  • Vertical Sync → Off (in-game) / Fast (in NVCP): Disable V-Sync in the game's settings. Enable "Fast" V-Sync in NVIDIA Control Panel instead. Fast Sync renders uncapped frames but only displays complete frames, giving you the latency benefit of uncapped FPS with zero screen tearing. Requires your FPS to exceed your monitor's refresh rate, so pair with an FPS cap (see below).
  • Max Frame Rate → Monitor Refresh Rate minus 3: For a 144Hz monitor, cap at 141 FPS. This keeps you inside G-Sync range (G-Sync disengages at or above max refresh rate) and prevents V-Sync latency penalty from ever engaging.
  • Shader Cache Size → 10 GB or Unlimited: Mortal Shell II compiles shaders on first launch and caches them. A larger cache prevents shader recompilation stutter when you revisit areas after driver updates.

AMD Adrenalin Settings

AMD users should configure the following in Adrenalin Software → Gaming → Mortal Shell II:

  • Radeon Anti-Lag → Enabled: AMD's equivalent of NVIDIA Reflex/Ultra Low Latency. Reduces input lag by ~30% at 60 FPS. Essential for parry timing.
  • Radeon Image Sharpening → 20–30%: Offsets the softening effect of FSR or TSR upscaling. Do not exceed 30% — higher values introduce haloing artifacts around character silhouettes against the game's dark backgrounds.
  • Surface Format Optimization → Enabled: Slight VRAM optimization that improves texture-streaming efficiency. No visual quality impact.
  • Tessellation Mode → Override Application Settings → 8x or 16x: Mortal Shell II uses moderate tessellation on terrain and shell armor details. Capping at 8x prevents the engine from over-tessellating distant geometry you cannot see, saving ~4–8% GPU time in open areas like Fallgrim.
  • VRAM Tuning → Fast Timing (for RX 6000/7000 series): In the Tuning tab, set VRAM timing to "Fast Timing" if your card is stable. Improves memory bandwidth by ~3–5%, which helps with the game's heavy texture-streaming demands during zone transitions.

Stutter & Frame-Time Fixes

UE5 games are notorious for traversal stutter and shader compilation hitches. Mortal Shell II is better than most, but these issues still exist. Here is the complete fix checklist, ordered from most to least impactful:

1. Shader Pre-Compilation (Do This First)

Mortal Shell II compiles shaders when you first launch the game after installation or after a driver update. Do not skip this step. Let the shader compilation bar finish completely before starting a game. If you skip it, every new visual effect (boss particle, environmental shader, weapon enchantment glow) will hitch the first time it appears — and you do NOT want a 200ms freeze when Imrod does his first Lava Call.

If you already skipped it: delete the shader cache folder at %LOCALAPPDATA%\MortalShell2\Saved\ShaderCache\ (Windows) or ~/.local/share/MortalShell2/Saved/ShaderCache/ (Linux/Steam Deck) and relaunch the game to trigger recompilation.

2. Disable Control Flow Guard (CFG) for the Executable

Windows Control Flow Guard adds ~5–8% CPU overhead to UE5 games. To disable it for Mortal Shell II only:

  1. Open Windows Security → App & Browser Control → Exploit Protection Settings
  2. Click Program Settings → Add Program to Customize → Choose Exact File Path
  3. Navigate to mortalshell2.exe (usually C:\Program Files (x86)\Steam\steamapps\common\Mortal Shell II\Binaries\Win64\)
  4. Find Control Flow Guard (CFG) → check "Override system settings" → set to Off
  5. Restart the game
Security Note: Disabling CFG only for this specific executable carries minimal risk. Do NOT disable it system-wide. Mortal Shell II is a single-player game without network attack surface beyond Steam's matchmaking layer.

3. Windows Game Mode & Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling

  • Game Mode: ON (Windows Settings → Gaming → Game Mode). Game Mode prioritizes CPU and GPU resources for the foreground game and suppresses background Windows Update and notification processes.
  • Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling (HAGS): ON (Windows Settings → System → Display → Graphics → Default Graphics Settings). HAGS reduces frame-time variance by ~12% in DX12 UE5 titles. If you use DLSS Frame Generation (RTX 40 series), HAGS is required — Frame Gen will not function without it.

4. Engine.ini Tweaks (Advanced)

Add these lines to %LOCALAPPDATA%\MortalShell2\Saved\Config\WindowsNoEditor\Engine.ini (create the file if it doesn't exist):

[SystemSettings]
r.Streaming.PoolSize=0
r.Streaming.LimitPoolSizeToVRAM=1
r.Streaming.MaxTempMemoryAllowed=256
r.OneFrameThreadLag=1
r.D3D12.UseAllowTearing=1
r.VSync=0

[/script/engine.renderersettings]
r.CreateShadersOnLoad=1
r.ShaderPipelineCache.Enabled=1
r.ShaderPipelineCache.BackgroundBatchSize=0
r.ShaderPipelineCache.BatchSize=50
r.ShaderPipelineCache.BackgroundBatchTime=0.0

What each line does: The streaming section adjusts UE5's texture-streaming behavior to use your GPU's full VRAM pool, reducing mid-traversal I/O spikes. The shader pipeline section forces all shaders to compile at load time instead of on-demand, eliminating in-game compilation stutter — at the cost of slightly longer initial loading screens.

Ultrawide & HDR Setup

Ultrawide Support (21:9 / 32:9)

Mortal Shell II supports 21:9 (3440×1440) natively — gameplay and cutscenes render correctly without stretching or cropping. 32:9 (5120×1440) users may encounter minor HUD misalignment: the health bar, stamina wheel, and Harden cooldown indicator drift slightly toward center-screen.

Fix for 32:9 HUD: Add the following to Engine.ini:

[/Script/Engine.UserInterfaceSettings]
ApplicationScale=1.0
UIScaleRule=ShortestSide
UIScaleCurve=(EditorCurveData=(Keys=((Time=720.0,Value=0.75),(Time=1080.0,Value=0.85),(Time=1440.0,Value=1.0),(Time=8650.0,Value=1.0))))

HDR Configuration

Mortal Shell II's HDR implementation uses scRGB color space. For the best results:

  • Enable HDR in Windows before launching the game (Display Settings → HDR → On)
  • In-game: set HDR Mode → On, HDR Brightness → 1000 nits (or your display's actual peak brightness)
  • HDR UI Brightness → 200–300 nits (prevents HUD burn-in on OLED displays)
  • Leave HDR Contrast at the default (1.0) — increasing it crushes blacks and hides detail in dark areas, which is most of the game

The game's dark atmosphere benefits tremendously from HDR. Fallgrim at night, the Crypt of Martyrs torches, and Corvid's Shroud of Darkness phase all show dramatic range expansion on an HDR display. If you have an OLED panel (LG C-series, Alienware AW3423DW, etc.), the near-infinite contrast ratio makes torch-lit dungeon crawls genuinely atmospheric.

Controller & Input Lag Optimization

Controller Choice

Mortal Shell II was designed for controller. Keyboard and mouse work but the Harden mechanic (default: Left Ctrl) and heavy attack (Shift+Click) are awkward placements for a mechanic you use every few seconds. Rebind immediately if you insist on KBM — recommended: Harden on Mouse Button 4, heavy attack on Mouse Button 5.

For controllers, PlayStation 5 DualSense and Xbox Series controllers both work natively via Steam Input. The DualSense has a slight edge: haptic feedback provides distinct vibration patterns for Harden impact, parry success, and low-health heartbeat — subtle but useful cues during chaotic boss fights.

Wireless vs. Wired

  • Wired: 1–2ms input latency. Use this for boss fights where frame-perfect parries matter.
  • Wireless (Bluetooth): 8–15ms input latency. Fine for exploration and mob clearing. The Xbox Wireless Adapter (proprietary 2.4GHz) sits at ~3–5ms — a good middle ground.
  • Steam Deck / Handheld: Built-in controller is optimal. Enable "Allow Tearing" in Steam Deck Quick Access Menu to reduce latency by ~1 frame.

Dead Zone Calibration

Default stick dead zones are too generous, creating a sluggish feel. In Controller Settings:

  • Left Stick Dead Zone: 5% (default 15%) — improves movement responsiveness without introducing stick drift
  • Right Stick Dead Zone: 3% (default 15%) — camera movement feels dramatically more responsive
  • Trigger Dead Zone: 0% — instant light attack on trigger pull
Pro Insight: If you are coming from Elden Ring or Dark Souls, Mortal Shell II's default camera sensitivity is about 30% lower. Bump Camera Sensitivity to 7 or 8 (default is 5) to match what your muscle memory expects. Nothing feels worse than losing a boss fight because your camera can't keep up with The Unchained's teleport dashes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What are the best Mortal Shell II PC graphics settings for 60 FPS on a mid-range GPU?

For a mid-range GPU (RTX 3060 / RX 6600 XT) at 1440p: set TSR or DLSS to Quality, Shadows to Medium, Global Illumination to High, Reflections to Medium, Volumetric Fog to Medium, Post-Processing to Medium, and Textures to Ultra. This yields a stable 62–72 FPS in demanding areas and 80+ FPS in interiors. Motion Blur and Depth of Field should both be OFF for better visual clarity during combat.

Q: How do I fix Mortal Shell II stuttering and frame drops on PC?

Stuttering in Mortal Shell II typically has three causes: shader compilation (fix by letting the initial shader pre-compilation finish completely), texture streaming from slow storage (ensure the game is installed on an SSD, preferably NVMe), and Windows Control Flow Guard overhead (disable CFG for mortalshell2.exe in Windows Exploit Protection settings). For persistent micro-stutter, add the Engine.ini tweaks in section 4 above and enable Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling in Windows.

Q: Does Mortal Shell II support NVIDIA DLSS and Frame Generation?

Yes, Mortal Shell II supports DLSS Super Resolution (Quality/Balanced/Performance/Ultra Performance modes) and DLSS Frame Generation on RTX 40 series GPUs. DLSS Quality at 1440p provides a ~35% FPS boost with minimal visual quality loss. Frame Generation adds an additional ~50% FPS but introduces 5–8ms of additional latency — enable it only if your base frame rate is already above 60 FPS, otherwise the latency penalty becomes noticeable in parry timing.

Q: How do I enable ultrawide 21:9 or 32:9 support in Mortal Shell II?

21:9 (3440×1440) works natively — simply select the resolution in the game's display settings. 32:9 (5120×1440) also works for gameplay but the HUD may drift toward center-screen. Fix this by adding UIScaleRule adjustments to Engine.ini (see the Ultrawide section above). Cutscenes render correctly at both aspect ratios without letterboxing.

Q: Why does my controller feel sluggish or have input lag in Mortal Shell II?

Start by reducing stick dead zones: set Left Stick Dead Zone to 5%, Right Stick Dead Zone to 3%, and Trigger Dead Zone to 0%. Increase Camera Sensitivity to 7 or 8 (default 5 is too slow). For NVIDIA users, enable Low Latency Mode → Ultra in NVIDIA Control Panel. For AMD users, enable Radeon Anti-Lag. Use a wired connection instead of Bluetooth to eliminate wireless latency. Lastly, disable in-game V-Sync and use NVIDIA Fast Sync or AMD Enhanced Sync instead.

Q: What are the Mortal Shell II minimum and recommended PC system requirements?

Minimum (1080p/30 FPS): Intel i5-8400 or Ryzen 5 2600, GTX 1060 6GB or RX 580 8GB, 16GB RAM, SATA SSD. Recommended (1440p/60 FPS): Intel i7-10700 or Ryzen 5 5600X, RTX 3060 Ti or RX 6700 XT, 16GB RAM, NVMe SSD. 4K/60 FPS requires a high-end build: Intel i7-13700K or Ryzen 7 7800X3D, RTX 4080 or RX 7900 XTX, 32GB DDR5, and a Gen4 NVMe SSD. An SSD is mandatory regardless of resolution — an HDD causes 3–8 second texture-streaming hitches during zone transitions.
